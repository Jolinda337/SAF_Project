Frequently Asked Questions

These are some of the Frequently asked Questions

1. What is Sexual Reproductive Health (SRH)?

Sexual Reproductive Health (SRH) refers to a state of physical, emotional, mental, and social well-being in relation to all aspects of sexuality and reproduction. It encompasses not only the absence of diseases, disabilities, or complications related to sexual and reproductive functions but also the ability to have safe, satisfying, and respectful sexual relationships. SRH includes access to information, education, services, and support that enable individuals to make informed decisions about their sexual and reproductive lives.

2. What is safe sex, and how can I practice it?

Safe sex refers to sexual activities that reduce the risk of sexually transmitted infections (STIs) and unintended pregnancies. To practice safe sex: Use barrier methods like condoms (male or female) to prevent STIs and unwanted pregnancies. Get regular STI screenings and encourage your partner(s) to do the same. Consider using other forms of contraception, such as birth control pills, patches, or intrauterine devices (IUDs) to prevent pregnancy.

3. How do I talk to my partner about using protection?

Open communication is key: Choose a comfortable and private setting to discuss the topic. Approach the conversation without judgment or blame. Explain the importance of protection for both partners' health and well-being. Listen to your partner's concerns and preferences. Explore different options together and decide on a method that works for both of you.

4. What should I do if I suspect I have an STI?

If you suspect you have an STI: Refrain from sexual activity to avoid spreading the infection. Consult a healthcare professional for testing and diagnosis. Inform any sexual partners so they can also get tested and treated if necessary. Follow the prescribed treatment plan and attend follow-up appointments.

5. How can I manage menstrual pain and discomfort?

To manage menstrual pain and discomfort: Use over-the-counter pain relievers like ibuprofen or naproxen. Apply heat to the lower abdomen to ease cramps. Stay hydrated and maintain a balanced diet. Engage in gentle exercise or relaxation techniques. Consider talking to a healthcare provider if pain is severe or disrupts daily activities.
